# Atech Arcade — The Pitch

## The 60-second pitch

> A **$229 Playdate plays alone.** A **$199 Tidbyt just sits there.**
> **This is $249 and does both** — a console you play *and* an ambient pixel display that earns its shelf space when you're not playing.
>
> It's two Atech boards that click together from stock modules, no soldering: a brain with a color scoreboard, a speaker and two knobs with LED rings, driving a 108-pixel light matrix that plays Pong, Snake, Tetris and a dozen more — then melts into a clock or a pixel-art scene when the game's over.
>
> Here's the part that matters to *you*: **every single unit sold moves a Motherboard, a Knob, a Speaker, a Screen — and twelve Light Grids.** Five module types in one purchase. It pulls your hardest-to-attach SKU through the door twelve at a time, opens up gamers, gift and classroom aisles you don't sell to today, and stacks a recurring game-pass business on top of the hardware.
>
> We've got working two-player Pong on real hardware and a margin model in your own numbers. **This is your flagship build-it-yourself console. Powered by Atech.**

## Three sharp value props

1. **Two gadgets, one price — the only one that does both.**
   Console *plus* ambient display. Playdate never lights your room; Tidbyt never lets you play; Divoom just loops GIFs. Atech Arcade is the only $200-class device that is genuinely both, which is why it can command Playdate-tier pricing while out-utilitizing a Tidbyt.

2. **A hardware razor with a software blade — built entirely from your catalog.**
   The Duo attaches **5 Atech module types and 12 Light Grids** in a single sale, and the **Arcade Pass / game packs** keep earning after the box ships. Hardware margin *and* recurring revenue, on a stack Atech owns end to end.

3. **Credible today, not vaporware.**
   Working two-player Pong on the real 108-px matrix, a loss-proof link protocol, ~10-minute tile calibration, and one dependency-free engine that also runs in a desktop simulator. We show a **working prototype and honest unit economics** — the WiFi link and game market are clearly labeled roadmap.

## Why this wins "Build for the Market" — with Atech as the judges

The brief is *build for the market*. **The judges are the market.** Most teams will demo a clever thing. We built a **business for the people holding the scorecards** — here's why it lands:

- **It's revenue for the judges.** The whole pitch is a P&L in Atech's own numbers: BOM, gross margin, SKU ladder, pull-through. We're not asking Atech to admire a toy — we're handing them a product line.

- **It pulls through the SKU nobody attaches.** A **Light Grid is a $9 impulse module** that no one buys twelve of — until they need a *screen*. The Duo needs exactly twelve, plus 2 Motherboards, 2 Knobs, a Screen and a Speaker. **One SKU, five module types, twelve of the hard one.**

- **It expands the market past makers.** Atech sells to builders today. Arcade is a **gift, a classroom kit, a desk toy, a gamer's gadget** — four new aisles for the *same modules*.

- **It's razor-and-blades.** The console sells once; the **Arcade Pass and game packs sell forever**, on a platform Atech already controls silicon-to-store.

- **Only Atech can build it — that's the moat.** At Atech *retail*, the Duo's parts cost **~$292** — no third party can make a $249 console from them. At Atech's *internal* cost the same box is **~$120–175**, i.e. **30–52% gross margin.** The economics *only* close as a first-party / licensed Atech product. **This is uniquely Atech's to own.**

- **It's a marketing asset even if it never ships.** A finished-feeling console is the single best demo of "look what clicks together, zero solder." It **sells kits just by existing** on the atech.dev homepage.

**The ask:** adopt Atech Arcade as Atech's flagship build-it-yourself console + ambient display. We bring the working build and the model; Atech brings the catalog, the cost base, and the storefront.

### Honesty line (say it out loud)

Working 2-player Pong exists **today**. The board-to-board link is **currently a laptop USB bridge** (native WiFi is written and in-tree, not switched on for the noisy demo room). The **game market and WiFi are roadmap.** We're showing a working prototype and credible economics — not a finished retail unit. That's the honest version, and it's still a business.
